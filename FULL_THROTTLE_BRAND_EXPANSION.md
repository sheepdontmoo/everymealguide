## Direction

Every Meal Guide should become the broadest practical consumer map of meal kits, prepared meals, meal prep, frozen meals, diet meal plans, and high-protein meal delivery across the United States, United Kingdom, Ireland, Australia, Canada, and then verified global markets.

The goal is not to claim every provider is recommended. The goal is to account for every provider with a clear status:

- active recommendation
- comparison-only
- regional provider
- watchlist
- inactive / legacy
- not enough evidence yet

## Commercial priority rules

P0:
Broad country coverage, high search intent, likely affiliate program, and clear consumer demand.

P1:
Strong regional or category intent, useful comparison target, or good affiliate potential.

P2:
Useful long-tail, diet-specific, local, or trust-building provider.

P3:
Tracked for coverage, not immediate monetization.

Blocked:
Inactive, legally risky, unsupported by current evidence, or should not be promoted.

## Evidence rules before a brand powers SEO pages

A brand-market row needs:

- official consumer-facing URL
- country or service-area evidence
- active menu, signup, ordering, delivery FAQ, or pricing signal
- source-backed category tags
- `public_claim_level`
- `commercial_status`
- evidence URL and check date

If any of those are missing, the brand can stay in research or watchlist status, but it should not become a recommendation or a "best" page candidate.

## Page-generation rules

- `best X in Y` pages need at least 3 verified eligible providers.
- Brand-vs-brand pages need overlapping country/category relevance.
- Alternatives pages need source-backed inactive, unavailable, or mismatch evidence.
- Diet and health-adjacent pages need neutral comparison language and source-backed plan details.
- Tables should support the page, not define the experience. Lead with shortlists, scenario cards, and answer-first copy.
